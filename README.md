The Camera Chromaticity & Gamut Mapping Tool is a self-contained, single-file HTML application that visualises the colour gamuts of professional digital cameras and compares them against industry-standard target colour spaces. It requires no server, no installation, and no network connection — open the HTML file in any modern browser.
The tool is built on the CIE 1931 xy chromaticity diagram and presents two complementary plot types: a traditional CIE 1931 xy diagram and an xy polar plot centred on the working white point. Camera characteristics are derived from DNG ForwardMatrix 2 values extracted with ExifTool.

The Colourspace Gamut Mapping Tool is a self-contained, single-file HTML application that visualises industry-standard colour spaces on the CIE 1931 xy chromaticity diagram and polar plot, and applies five distinct gamut mapping strategies to show how colours are transformed when converting from one colour space to another.

The Luminance-Aware Gamut Mapping Tool extends conventional CIE xy chromaticity gamut analysis into the perceptually uniform CIELAB colour space, adding the luminance dimension (Y) that xy-space tools cannot capture. Rather than treating primaries as pure chromaticity coordinates, it derives the full RGB→XYZ matrix for each colour space directly from its primary chromaticities and white point using the standard Colour Science method, extracting the Y luminance coefficients that describe how much each channel (R, G, B) contributes to overall image brightness — for sRGB these are the familiar 0.2126 / 0.7152 / 0.0722. Primaries are then converted to CIELAB and presented in three complementary views: the a*b* plane (hue and chroma), the L*C* plane (lightness vs saturation, the most diagnostic for luminance errors), and the L*h° polar plot (combined hue angle and lightness). For any source→target conversion across the tool's sixteen colour spaces, the analysis panel reports ΔL*, ΔC*, and Δh° per primary for both the native and mapped positions under five gamut mapping strategies, alongside a narrative identifying which channel carries the largest luminance-weighting difference, what that means for specific subject matter (skin tone, foliage and grey balance for green, sky and shadow for blue), and whether the chosen strategy resolves or leaves a residual lightness error — information entirely invisible to a pure xy analysis.
